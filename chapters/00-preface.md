Here are the answers:

**VC-backed** (tens of millions to billions in funding): Gamma ($87M raised, $2.1B valuation, ~50 employees), Canva ($570M+ raised, 5,000+ employees), Cursor ($2.3B raised, $29.3B valuation), Lovable ($330M raised, $6.6B valuation), Bolt.new ($105M raised), Pika ($55M+ raised), Descript ($101M raised), Opus Clip ($50M raised, backed by SoftBank), Photoroom ($64M raised), Jasper ($131M raised).

**One person or near-zero**: Makeform.ai (one solo founder), PhotoAI (one person — Pieter Levels — $1.6M/year), InteriorAI (same solo founder), Base44 (tiny team, built in months, sold for $80M), JotSlide.ai (micro-team).

---

Now here's the part that should unsettle you.

**You probably couldn't tell the difference.**

The products built by a single person in Chiang Mai look indistinguishable from the ones built by 300 engineers in San Francisco. The form builder made by a solo founder in a co-working space rivals the one made by 846 employees in a downtown headquarters. The AI photo tool run by one person from a laptop generates more revenue per employee than most Fortune 500 companies.

Some of these founders sit in the posh offices of **San Francisco**, **Hyderabad**, or **Hangzhou** — surrounded by venture capitalists, talent pipelines, and catered lunches. Others work from **Chiang Mai**, **Xi'an**, **Aachen**, or **Santiago (Chile)** — remote regions where the cost of living is low, the Wi-Fi is good enough, and the only colleague is a large language model.

Yet they ship the same product. They serve the same customers. They collect the same revenue.

**Why?**

Because in 2025, the marginal cost of building software collapsed to near zero. The tools that once required a 50-person engineering team — design, coding, testing, deployment, customer support, marketing copy — can now be orchestrated by AI agents that work 24 hours a day, never take vacation, and never ask for equity.

The question is no longer *"How many people do you need to build a company?"*

The question is: *"Do you need any people at all?"*

That is what this book is about.
